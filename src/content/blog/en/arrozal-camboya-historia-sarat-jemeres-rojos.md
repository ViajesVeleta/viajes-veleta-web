"When Pol Pot's **Khmer Rouge** arrived in Phnom Penh, they ordered an immediate evacuation. I ran out of my house with only what I was wearing: pants, a shirt, and my shoes." So begins the account from **Sarat**, our Cambodian guide, as we contemplate the serenity of the rice paddies.

Sarat tells us how he was taken to a concentration camp without fences, where fear was the only necessary border. There, the difference between life and death was measured in grains of rice: "Rice soup was water with a few floating grains; dry rice had a little more." For three years, he survived by eating ants, insects, and tree leaves until the war against Vietnam pushed the Khmer Rouge back into the mountains.

The most amazing thing about Sarat isn't just his story, but the infectious joy with which he explains his country to us today. In a nation where no family is untouched by the purges that wiped out a third of the population, the rice fields—like the one in the image—are not just the engine of their economy, but the stage where life, finally, sprouted again from the mud.
