**Geiranger: The Norwegian Fjord of Great Waterfalls**

Located on Norway's southern coast, it is visitable in all four seasons, though winter is for the bold.

Working without a day of rest for two and a half million years is bound to bear fruit. That's what the ice did on the Norwegian coast, leaving us with the greatest display of fjords in Europe and one of the most recognizable and spectacular landscapes in the world.

Choosing which of the Norwegian fjords is more beautiful is a vain hobby. They all rank high on the scale of spectacularity. However, some are more famous than others. Foreign travelers often rush to discover the Pulpit Rock in Lysefjord or its Southern neighbors, and in their anxiety, they might forget to call at Geiranger, located slightly further north.

Geiranger is touched by the wand of the rugged Norse gods. It is an inlet that twists and turns, playing at separating itself from the open sea. The glaucous water at the bottom contrasts with the dizzying streams falling from a succession of impressive waterfalls: the Seven Sisters (De syv søstrene), the Suitor (Friaren), or the Bridal Veil (Brudesløret), which gets its name because its stream breaks into millions of drops until it practically becomes a vertical mist. On sunny summer days—which are more frequent than many would suspect for Scandinavia—the air currents moved by the spray form successions of rainbows, in a perfect, almost invented postcard.

It seemed inevitable that, with all these graces to its credit, Geiranger would be targeted by UNESCO to be declared a World Heritage Site, an award it received in 2005 along with the Naerøy inlet.
