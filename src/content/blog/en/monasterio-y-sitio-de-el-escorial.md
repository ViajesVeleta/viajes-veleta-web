**Monastery and Site of El Escorial**

Today I leave you with one of the places described by **Eduardo Manzano** in his article \"Journey through Spain through 14 monuments.\"

“El Escorial cannot be understood without the contribution of the wealth arriving from the Americas. Its cold and austere tone should not make us forget that large sums were invested here, which also allowed the immense work to be completed in barely two decades. It was not, however, an imposing palace for the world's most powerful man, Philip II; it was a monastery, a place of retreat and meditation, where surrounded by close collaborators and Hieronymite monks, and in contact with God, the king sought inspiration to know how to manage the largest empire of the time. El Escorial reflects the character of the Spanish Empire at its peak, but also its decline. After the death of Philip II, the site was not much liked by his successors. It remained as the burial place for monarchs, though not always, and went through difficult times, such as the 19th-century confiscation, when it was nearly abandoned and its library suffered several lootings.”
