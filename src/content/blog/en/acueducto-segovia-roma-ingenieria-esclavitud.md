The **Segovia Aqueduct** is one of the most fascinating engineering works bequeathed to us by the Roman Empire on the Iberian Peninsula. Most surprising, beyond its imposing arches, is its excellent state of preservation despite being built towards the end of the 1st century AD.

The longevity of this monument is due to the extraordinary practical sense of the Romans: when they built something, they did so to last and to be useful to the community. In fact, the aqueduct remained in use until just a few years ago. Despite myths suggesting they didn't understand the principle of communicating vessels, the reality is that they simply lacked the piping technology to withstand the high pressures required for steep terrain. Thus, they chose the elegance of dry-stone masonry and the labor of legions of slaves to raise this structure which today, often silhouetted by hot air balloons during festivals, remains the heart and soul of Segovia.
