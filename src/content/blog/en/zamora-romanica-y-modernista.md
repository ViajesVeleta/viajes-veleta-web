**Zamora, Romanesque and Modernist**

The [Zamora Cathedral](https://catedralzamora.com/) is the symbol of this city, with its dome covered in "scales" overlooking the [Duero River](https://elpais.com/diario/2008/04/05/viajero/1207429692_850215.html). It sits at the edge of a city that takes us into the Middle Ages and also into Modernism. The Rúa de los Francos, lined with Romanesque churches, links the cathedral with Santa Clara Street, where Modernist buildings take over. Yet in all streets, the tranquility is almost total, except in bars during peak tapas hours, with [Toro wine](https://elpais.com/gastronomia/beber/2024-11-23/seis-grandes-vinos-tintos-de-toro-entre-la-potencia-y-la-finura.html) as the star pairing with all kinds of skewers (pinchos).

Another perspective is from the [Castle](https://elpais.com/diario/2010/02/06/viajero/1265494089_850215.html), which offers the best views over the cathedral, with its Byzantine-style ribbed dome and the Savior's Tower. Inside, a fantastic main altarpiece awaits and, best of all, the incredible Flemish tapestry museum. Here, around [the cathedral](https://catedralzamora.com/museo/), are the most photogenic spots, like Troncoso Street, narrow and with high stone walls, or the Troncoso viewpoint, with perfect views over the Duero and its Romanesque bridge. In this medieval core, there are nearly 20 Romanesque churches, such as San Pedro and San Ildefonso, with a Virgin of Beautiful Love whose earrings are changed twice a year. Or the church of Santa María Magdalena, in the Rúa de los Francos, one of the most elegant. Or San Juan Bautista, in the Plaza Mayor, with impressive arches and an impeccable Mudejar ceiling.

The Plaza Mayor and the [Parador](https://paradores.es/es/parador-de-zamora), with a splendid cloister, complete the essential landmarks on this path, as is having some pinchos in the area and continuing down the street to find [the main Modernist buildings of the city](https://turismo-zamora.com/ruta-del-modernismo.html), which were born during the urban boom experienced with the arrival of the railway. Most are on Balborraz Street (Faustina House, or Mariano López's, for example) and on Santa Clara Street (the houses of Valentín Matilla, Francisco Antón, Valentín Guerra, or Félix Galarza).
