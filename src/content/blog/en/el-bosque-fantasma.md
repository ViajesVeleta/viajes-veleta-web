The story of the **“Ghost Forest”**

The **“ghost forests”** in Australia, specifically those related to koalas, are wooded areas that have suffered severe defoliation—the loss of their leaves—due to koala overpopulation. Trees that would normally be green and lush are left bare, with skeletal branches and pale trunks, giving them a whitish or silvery appearance. This phenomenon is particularly noticeable in eucalyptus forests.

This occurs because koalas have a very specialized diet; they feed almost exclusively on eucalyptus leaves. In some regions of Australia, where natural predators have been eliminated and hunting is prohibited, koala populations can grow unchecked. When there are too many koalas for the number of trees available, the pressure on the food supply becomes unsustainable. Koalas eat the leaves faster than the trees can regenerate them, leaving behind landscapes like the one seen in the image.

Photograph: taken during the journey along the **Great Ocean Road** at the **Ghost Forest Cape Otway**.

The **Great Ocean Road** is an iconic 243 km coastal route located in Victoria, Australia. Famous for its breathtaking ocean views, this road connects the towns of Torquay and Allansford. Along the way, you can visit the famous **Twelve Apostles** (in Port Campbell National Park), explore the **Great Otway National Park**, and spot koalas in their natural habitat.
