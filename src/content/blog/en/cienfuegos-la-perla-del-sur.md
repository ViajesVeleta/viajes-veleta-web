**Cienfuegos, a beautiful maritime and cultural city**

On the southern coast of central Cuba, next to one of the best bays in the world, lies the city of **Cienfuegos**, also the chief municipality of the province of the same name. It was officially founded as Villa de Fernandina de Jagua on April 22, 1819, by French settlers from Bordeaux in areas of an ancient aboriginal chiefdom, even though a Spanish presence had existed in the area since 1745. Its inhabitants pride themselves on living in one of the most beautiful and cleanest cities on the island.

The French presence overflows in almost all aspects of Cienfuegos life, while never ceasing to be, look, and behave like pure Cubans. The architecture is one of the most evident elements. Today, notable heritage buildings endure: palaces, churches, parks, theaters, a triumphal arch similar to Paris's, the Paseo del Prado that extends towards the sea, and wide streets in a perfect rectilinear layout. Everything fits into a strong eclecticism, where Mudéjar and Byzantine styles meet Venetian, Gothic, Baroque, and neoclassical ornamentation, creating a true architectural gem that has earned the epithet "The Pearl of the South."

The Historic Center of **Cienfuegos**, like those of other cities on the island, has a colonial-style urban core centered around the Plaza de Armas, today José Martí Park. The most important buildings were erected around it.
