On November 1st, the **Grand Egyptian Museum** officially and fully opens its doors, an essential visit for any trip to Cairo. Here are some facts about the museum, along with a photo we took during our tour with the **Viajes Veleta** group at the beginning of September.

**Location.** It is located on the outskirts of Cairo, very close to the **Pyramids of Giza** (Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure). Its 1 km long facade is perfectly aligned with the pyramids, echoing the symbolism used by the ancient Egyptians.

**Construction.** The construction of the GEM, which began in 2004, has lasted more than two decades (to compare, it's estimated the Great Pyramid of Giza took between 15 and 30 years). The investment has been nearly **1 billion euros**.

**Highlights.** The GEM is designed to house the **complete Tutankhamun collection** (more than 5,000 pieces) for the first time, displayed chronologically. It will also house the **Solar Boat of Khufu**.

**Dimensions.** It is the world's largest archaeological museum dedicated to a single civilization. It features an exhibition space of approximately **100,000 m²**, with the capacity to display around **15,000 pieces**.

**Exhibition Areas.** The museum consists of: **Main Galleries**, the **Grand Hall** (where the monumental statue of Ramesses II stands), the **Grand Staircase** (with statues of pharaohs), the Tutankhamun galleries, and the Solar Boat Museum.

**Impact.** The Egyptian government considers the GEM a "gift to the world" and a key milestone. Its opening is expected to generate a significant increase in tourism, with a forecast of reaching **30 million visitors** by 2028.

**Positano, where you can live the charm of the Amalfi Coast**

**Positano** is located west of the fascinating natural balcony over the Tyrrhenian Sea that is the Amalfi Coast, declared a World Heritage Site by UNESCO. Here reigns the typical Mediterranean landscape of southern Italy, with white houses sloping toward the sea and splendid beaches, alleys offering all the little pleasures of life: a slow pace, refined boutiques, cafes, and restaurants in these places that are guardians of history.

**A Unique Marvel in Luxor: Temple of Hatshepsut**

In the heart of Luxor, ancient Thebes, lies one of the most imposing and enigmatic monuments of Ancient Egypt: The Temple of Hatshepsut. This mortuary temple, known in its time as Djeser-Djeseru ("The Sublime of the Sublimes"), is considered a jewel of Egyptian architecture for its innovative design, strategic location, and historical relevance.

Situated in the Deir el-Bahari necropolis, at the foot of the limestone cliffs on the western bank of the Nile, the temple integrates perfectly with the desert landscape, creating a visual spectacle that fascinates archaeologists, travelers, and history lovers.

What makes this monument even more special is that it was built under the orders of Hatshepsut, one of the few women who ruled Egypt as pharaoh. Through its architecture and reliefs, the temple reflects not only the religiosity and art of Ancient Egypt but also the strength and political vision of a woman who challenged millenary traditions.

Visiting the Temple of Hatshepsut is to step into a story of power, ambition, spirituality, and beauty, where the memory of a queen who wanted to be pharaoh lives on after more than 3,000 years.

Among all the temples in Thailand, there is one that stands out for completely breaking away from the canons of traditional architecture expected in worship centers dedicated to Buddhism and Hinduism. This is the case of the unconventional temple **Wat Rong Khun**, whose name translates as "center of the city". It is located in the city of Chiang Rai, in the north of the country. This architectural work was created by the Thai visual artist Chalermchai Kositpipat, who often fuses Buddhist and Hindu imagery with elements of pop culture. **National Geographic.**
